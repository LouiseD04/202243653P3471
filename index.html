Story 1: Looking to the Future, Yet Leaving It In The Past

For over a decade now, the SFA (Scottish Football Association) has flown the flag for producing talented youth footballers. They advocate that youth players aged 12-16 should be given core development and the facilities to do so to achieve their footballing potential. To this end performance schools opened their doors in 2012, however just over a decade later these schools are being mothballed. Following this ambitious programme to nurture youth talent, why are these schools now being phased out.

What are JD Performance Schools?

In 2012, the SFA decided to employ a new strategy to the develop talented youth players. In partnership with seven local authority schools across Scotland, they aspired to create facilities where youngsters could be educated whilst also gaining valuable minutes on the pitch. Thus performance schools were born which were designed to give talented young male and female footballers the opportunity to realise their ambition of playing professionally.

Each performance school helps develop a pool of young footballers. Following a rigorous selection process, performance schools hand-pick players under the age of 12 to join their programme as they shift from primary to high school.

Over a four-year period of secondary education, pupils gain up to 800 extra hours of coaching per year. Every school has a designated elite youth coach who is supported by national team and club coaches alike, who all contribute to the players’ progression and success.

Geographically, pupils are most commonly selected from their local area and attend one of the performance schools within travelling distance of their home.

Why kick off Performance Schools

In 2010, Former First Minister Henry McLeish launched an independent review into Scottish football which recommended that performance schools be set up to hone future talent.

McLeish launched his review after the perpetual failings of the men’s national football team to qualify for major tournaments. This addressed a growing concern that Scotland wasn’t producing enough young footballing talent.

The Goal

via GIPHY

The aim of these performance schools is to produce young players who possess the potential to go on to play for Scottish clubs and the Scottish National Team at youth levels. Coaches in these schools train and develop young players who can adapt to the modern game through sharp thinking, immaculate passing; and who enhance an ever-changing field of play.

Everyday Training

In addition to their studies, pupils receive between 75 and 90 minutes of training every day.

A select few of these players not only attend the performance schools, they are also part of SPFL club academies with teams such as Motherwell, Rangers and Celtic. Attendance at these academies also involves training sessions and at least one game per week. An accumulation of training at an academy (270 minutes), coupled with the performance school (630 minutes), totals 900 minutes of football weekly.

Who has Attended Them?

Over the years, performance schools have witnessed thousands of pupils come through their ranks needless to say not everyone goes on to play professionally. That’s not to say, though, they haven’t honed the skills of many who have gone on to play professionally. The already mentioned, Billy Gilmour attended a performance school, as had other major names such as Bologna midfielder, Lewis Ferguson and Derby County defender, Max Johnston.

Success Rate

On a positive note, 50.7% of those who attended any JD performance school have gone on to play professionally, whether that be in the Scottish Premiership or Scottish divisions as well as Premier League and abroad.

Where performance schools have been least successful is in the number who have gone on to represent Scotland at the Under-21 level. Only 8.36% of graduates have represented Scotland at this level, which upholds Henry McLeish’s concern from 2010 of not enough talent coming through the Scotland ranks.

There have only ever been two females graduates of these schools who have played professionally, which makes up 4.02% of attendees. Although this figure is extremely low hinting at a lack of female prospects coming through, the two aforementioned women, Leah Eddie and Emma Watson, have become successful in their own right.

While these performance schools have been successful in kickstarting careers, the number of graduates has fallen nearly every year, with the exceptions coming in 2017 and again in 2022, when three age cohorts graduated at the same time due to postponements as a result of COVID.
